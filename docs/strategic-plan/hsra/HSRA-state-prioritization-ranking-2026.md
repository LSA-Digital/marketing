# HSRA State Prioritization Ranking 2026

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-12

## Purpose

This document replaces the earlier outreach-first ranking with a `need-first` ranking model for HSRA / PopHealthMap. The revised logic reflects a core product truth: states that are resource-poor, under-tooled, and still expected to make defensible county- and tract-level decisions are often the places where HSRA is most valuable.

The ranking still considers adoption readiness and access path, but those are now treated as execution filters rather than the dominant source of priority.

## Important Interpretation Rule

This ranking is `not` saying lower-ranked states do not matter. It is saying:

- all 50 states should still receive launch messaging
- the top-ranked states deserve the fastest research, strongest state-specific tailoring, and earliest discovery effort
- lower-ranked states should stay in launch coverage and nurture until new evidence or real campaign behavior changes the picture

## Methodology

### Three-lens model

Following the re-ranking request, each state is now assessed on three separate lenses:

1. `Need severity` - how strongly the state shows the public-health, rural, disparity, or targeting problem HSRA solves
2. `Adoption readiness` - whether the state has enough planning or institutional structure to absorb a state-first HSRA motion
3. `Access path` - whether the campaign can identify plausible owners, routes, collaborators, or proof-transfer paths

### Why the model changed

The earlier ranking over-rewarded states with existing dashboards, public portals, or configured analytics evidence. That was useful for predicting near-term messaging familiarity, but it incorrectly pushed down states with the strongest unmet need.

Under the revised model:

- a severe `capacity gap` is a positive need signal
- low technology sophistication is not treated as evidence of weak product fit
- access difficulty still matters, but it no longer cancels out extreme need

### Scoring emphasis

The revised weighting is directional rather than numerically rigid:

| Lens | Working emphasis | What it captures |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Need severity | 50% | burden, capacity gap, funding / planning pressure, disparity urgency |
| Adoption readiness | 30% | enough institutional structure to absorb a state-first deployment |
| Access path | 20% | named roles, routing clarity, academic or partner bridge, internal proof transfer |

### Need severity components

| Component | Weight inside need severity | Evidence used |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Rural burden and coverage pressure | 35% | rural hospital vulnerability, rural outcome burden, Medicaid non-expansion pressure |
| Capacity gap / transformational upside | 25% | visible need with limited existing analytics or burden-screening infrastructure |
| Public-health planning or funding pressure | 20% | SHA, SHIP, CHA, CHIP, PHAB, CHNA, RHT implementation need |
| Cancer / screening / disparity urgency | 20% | cancer-control plans, screening gaps, equity and targeting pressure |

### Adoption readiness components

| Component | Weight inside adoption readiness | Evidence used |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Institutional foothold | 40% | state public-health structure, health-equity or planning programs, PHAB or analogous activity |
| Message-lane clarity | 35% | whether a clear state-specific HSRA story is obvious from public evidence |
| Adjacent configured-analytics evidence | 25% | dashboard builds, CHNA / CHA support, or state analytics analogs |

### Access-path components

| Component | Weight inside access path | Evidence used |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Named owner or routing clarity | 50% | identifiable state office, planning lead, or likely routing path |
| Academic / partner bridge | 30% | university, collaborator, or partner path that strengthens credibility |
| Internal proof-transfer value | 20% | Tennessee story transfer, existing relationships, or adjacent narrative fit |

### Confidence labels

| Confidence | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| High | 3 or more recent sources across multiple signal types |
| Medium | 2 useful sources or mixed evidence strength |
| Low | mostly baseline funding signal or limited differentiated evidence |

## Evidence Base Used

This ranking uses the following evidence categories already gathered in repo research plus targeted state-source checks:

- official CMS FY2026 RHT award amounts for all 50 states
- rural hospital vulnerability and rural burden signals from Chartis and related public-health reference sources
- official or public state health-equity, disparity, and planning infrastructure signals
- official SHA / SHIP / county-indicator or state assessment infrastructure
- public cancer-control, screening-gap, and disparity evidence from the deeper state framework review
- public CHNA / CHA or analytics-procurement analogs where relevant
- internal commercial anchor evidence for Tennessee
- `docs/research/hsra/hsra-state-outreach-priority-framework-2026.md`

## Why Some States Moved Up

### States elevated by the need-first rerank

1. `Mississippi`
   - severe burden and capacity gap now count as a strong HSRA need case rather than a weak maturity signal
2. `Alabama`
   - rural hospital stress and non-expansion pressure now outweigh the lack of higher-end public analytics infrastructure
3. `Georgia`
   - burden, non-expansion pressure, and current cancer-plan activity together create a stronger urgency story than the earlier model recognized
4. `Arkansas`
   - the highest rural hospital vulnerability rate in the country is now treated as a major strategic signal
5. `West Virginia` and `Kansas`
   - both rise because severe burden and under-tooling now matter more than dashboard maturity

### States that moved down under the new logic

- `Colorado`, `Connecticut`, `Minnesota`, and `Oregon` remain good HSRA targets, but they now rank below the severe unmet-need states because they already show stronger analytics maturity or institutional tooling.
- `Pennsylvania` remains a strong planning and assessment state, but it is less urgent from a capacity-gap perspective than the burden-led states now ahead of it.
- `New York` remains in Tier A as an intentional special case because of its strategic signaling value, scale, and unusually important reporting and hospital-planning environment.
- `Massachusetts` sits in Tier B because its equity and EJ maturity still create a meaningful HSRA story even though it is not a severe unmet-need state.
- `California` and `Washington` move into Tier B as strategic bridge states because partnership routes and the team's better odds of in-person buyer meetings in the NY / MA / CA / WA corridor improve access quality enough to justify faster follow-up.

## Recommended Use In Campaign Planning

- `Launch to all 50 states` remains the right top-of-funnel move.
- `Prioritize Tier A first` for the next state-packet buildout and the strongest state-specific message tailoring.
- `Use Tier B as the bridge cohort` for states that may be easier to route because of partner paths, travel access, or in-person meeting odds even if their unmet need is somewhat lower.
- `Keep Tier C in broad launch coverage and nurture` until either public evidence improves or real campaign behavior proves stronger receptivity.

## What Still Needs More Research

The ranking is now better aligned to HSRA's real value proposition, but it still needs two follow-on overlays:

1. a `commercial access path` overlay based on named contacts, partner routes, and real routing quality
2. a `campaign response` overlay based on actual outreach behavior rather than public proxies alone

Highest-value next research moves:

1. build or refresh state packets for the newly elevated need-first Tier A states
2. add a contact-quality and access-path layer to the working matrix
3. test campaign response data against this ranking and revise quarterly
