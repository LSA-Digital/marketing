**CONNECTICUT LAW REVIEW**

**VOLUME 26, WINTER 1994, NUMBER 2**

**Trashing the Town and Making It Pay: The Problem with the Municipal Liability Scheme Under CERCLA**

G. Nelson Smith, III\*-----I. INTRODUCTION

In 1980, the Environmental Protection Agency ("EPA") estimated that there were approximately 30,000 to 50,000 hazardous waste sites existing in the United States. Of these, 1200 to 2000 posed a serious risk to public health. In response to this situation, Congress passed the Comprehensive Environmental Response Compensation and Liability Act ("CERCLA"), which established a $1.6 billion cleanup fund (the "Superfund"), financed in equal amounts by industry-based fees and federal appropriations. The purpose of establishing the fund was to enable the government to clean up high-risk sites quickly and thus eliminate serious public health and safety threats. The legislation also established a federal cause of action that allowed the EPA to pursue persons responsible for the contamination of a site and hold them strictly liable for the costs incurred by the government in the cleanup. The goal of CERCLA was to persuade individuals responsible for contaminating a site to pursue voluntarily appropriate remedial actions.

When a site is proposed to be cleaned up in accordance with CERCLA, it is placed on the National Priorities List ("NPL"). While most of those held responsible for the cleanup of the sites placed on the NPL are private parties, municipalities are also often responsible. Of the 1219 proposed and final NPL sites in 1989, for example, 320 (approximately 25%) were identified by the EPA as involving waste from municipalities. The EPA also classified approximately 236 of the sites as municipal landfills.

In recent years, there has been a great deal of debate as to whether municipalities may be held legally responsible under CERCLA for cleaning up landfills. That issue seems to have been resolved, at least for the moment, by two federal court decisions, *B.F. Goodrich Co. v. Murtha* and *Transportation Leasing Co. v. California*, both of which determined that municipalities can be held liable under CERCLA. Other scholars have rather eloquently attacked the pros and cons of municipal liability under CERCLA. However, whether or not liability is established under CERCLA is only a subset of the fundamental problems that arise when liability is apportioned in any case involving municipalities. Starting with the assumption that municipalities are liable under CERCLA, the purpose of this Article is to point out the consequences of plastering the "liability" label on municipalities and to propose a more appropriate way of handling municipal liability under CERCLA.II. THE CERCLA LIABILITY SCHEMEA. Background

All wastes, regardless of their properties (i.e., solid, liquid, or gas) are characterized as "solid" waste. Some solid wastes are considered "sanitary" while others are considered "hazardous". CERCLA's definition of "hazardous" is quite broad. Section 101(14) of CERCLA deems hazardous any element, compound, mixture, solution, or substance so designated by the EPA. So far, the EPA has listed over 700 substances as hazardous.

Under CERCLA, municipal solid waste (“MSW”), which includes household waste, is generally considered hazardous. The waste normally contains hazardous substances which commingle with sanitary waste. As a result, the waste must be disposed of in sanitary landfills. Municipalities are the entities generally responsible for the collection and disposal of MSW in landfills. It is through the release of hazardous substances from landfills that municipalities are affected by the provisions of CERCLA.

Prior to the passage of CERCLA in 1980, the EPA had no authority to hold municipalities responsible for the cleanup of hazardous waste. The key environmental legislation in place prior to CERCLA was the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act ("RCRA"), which was passed in 1976\. While RCRA governed the handling of hazardous waste from cradle to grave, it exempted, for the most part, MSW from its definition of hazardous waste. Thus, municipalities were able to avoid liability resulting from the disposal of MSW. Because MSW is normally deemed hazardous under CERCLA, however, the EPA now has the authority to hold municipalities responsible for cleaning up hazardous waste sites.

In pursuing those responsible for cleaning up a hazardous waste site, the EPA may sue four types of parties:

* (a) present owners and operators of a facility;  
* (b) past owners and operators of a facility;  
* (c) persons who generated or arranged for disposal or treatment of hazardous substance; and  
* (d) transporters of hazardous substances.

For municipalities, liability generally attaches under the third category: the arranging for disposal or treatment of a hazardous substance. Two recent cases have confirmed that municipalities can be held liable under this provision.

*Transportation Leasing Co. v. California* arose out of a suit brought by the EPA against a company for response costs incurred as a result of abating various hazardous conditions at a landfill. The claims were settled in a partial consent decree, and the company agreed to pay the State of California and the EPA approximately $61 million. The company reserved the right to seek indemnification against other potentially responsible parties who were not signatories to the decree and subsequently sought contribution from a number of entities, including twenty-nine municipalities and the County of Los Angeles. The company claimed that the defendants either owned, operated, or utilized the landfill. In determining that the municipalities were potentially liable, the court held that by contracting for the disposal of the cities' waste, the cities arranged for disposal within the meaning of CERCLA.

Similarly, in *B.F. Goodrich Co. v. Murtha*, the owners/operators of two landfills in Connecticut were sued by the EPA for cleanup costs. The owners commenced third-party actions against approximately two hundred parties and several Connecticut municipal defendants. The Second Circuit, affirming the district court's holding that municipalities can be liable under CERCLA, stated:Liability under CERCLA depends only on the presence in any form of listed hazardous substances. Hence, if a municipality arranges for the disposal or treatment of waste containing substances listed as hazardous it may be held liable for contribution or response costs under the Act if a subsequent release or threatened release requires cleanup efforts.

Despite the fact that *B.F. Goodrich* and *Transportation Leasing* have established that municipalities may be held liable under CERCLA, a recent decision arising out of the *B.F. Goodrich* litigation ("*B.F. Goodrich II*") provides some encouraging news for municipalities. While the court acknowledged that municipalities are potentially liable under CERCLA, it refused to impose liability absent proof that the municipality's MSW contained a hazardous substance "particularly identified" under section 101(14) of CERCLA. The court thus rejected the proposition that since MSW as a general rule contains hazardous substances, a municipality can be liable under CERCLA based on this fact alone. The court held that "\[l\]iability does not lie if the material disposed of would release a \[hazardous substance\] only on the intervention of another force," and further stated that "\[t\]he fact that waste contains items which were made with, by use of, or incorporated components or elements which constituted or in turn contained \[hazardous substances\] is not a sufficient basis for finding that disposal of such waste constitutes disposal of \[hazardous waste\]". Citing an EPA policy, the court concluded that a municipality should not be charged as a responsible party "absent evidence that its waste deposited at the site in question contained specific \[hazardous substances\]".

Under *B.F. Goodrich II*, a party seeking contribution from a municipality must provide sufficient proof that the MSW of the municipality contained specific hazardous substances. While this heightened evidentiary standard makes it more difficult to hold municipalities liable, it does not make municipalities immune from liability. As long as this heightened standard is satisfied, municipalities are still liable.B. Problems with the Present CERCLA Scheme

**1\. To Sue or Not To Sue: The EPA's Dilemma**

The determination that municipalities can be held liable under CERCLA has had far-ranging consequences. On one hand, if municipalities fail to arrange for disposal of waste, they create a potential health risk. On the other hand, if municipalities do arrange for disposal, they potentially face millions of dollars in liability. The legislative history of CERCLA states that the Act's purpose is "to protect public health and environment from the dangers posed by such sites". In furtherance of this goal, however, municipalities often are punished disproportionately by having to pay high damage awards resulting from the CERCLA liability scheme. Moreover, as the law is currently interpreted, there is very little municipalities can do to avoid liability, other than to hope that the waste deposited in landfills is not hazardous. Unfortunately, in most instances, this is not the case. As one author has noted:The home garbage pail is a leaking sieve of toxic and potentially toxic chemical agents. Some of these chemicals are contained in the pesticides, paints, degreasers, preservatives, detergents, oven cleaners, insecticides, and even shampoos sold ubiquitously over the counter to consumers. Other elements in household waste can become hazardous during the decay and decomposition process. Either alone or in chemical consort with other wastes in a landfill, the chemical outflow from the typical household is a ticking toxic time bomb.... Telephone surveys reveal that the most commonly used and discarded hazardous household substances in descending order, are household cleaners, pesticides, auto and furniture polishes, paint and paint thinners, motor oil, chemical drain openers, antifreeze, wood preservatives, and herbicides.

Paint products, solvents, batteries, pesticides and herbicides, adhesive, aerosols, alcohols, oil and grease, polishes and waxes, cosmetics, and dyes containing dozens of metals as well as halogenated and nonhalogenated compounds are also found in municipal landfills, and all qualify as hazardous substances under CERCLA.

To eliminate some of the confusion that both CERCLA and RCRA caused in determining the classification of municipal solid waste, the EPA has established a "Municipal Settlement Policy" which sets forth its position on municipal liability under CERCLA. The policy states that CERCLA:does not provide an exemption from liability for municipal wastes. Municipal wastes may be considered hazardous substances if they are covered under the definition of hazardous substances in section 101(14) of CERCLA. . . . To the extent municipal wastes contain a hazardous substance that is covered under section 101(14) of CERCLA and there is a release or threatened release, such municipal wastes may fall within the CERCLA liability framework.

However, the EPA announced in the same policy statement that, unless a truly exceptional circumstance exists, it would not pursue municipalities or private parties who are generators/transporters of MSW if the MSW contains only household hazardous waste. Unfortunately, the EPA's position on this issue only confuses both municipalities and private parties as to the potential liabilities associated with the disposal of MSW.

The EPA has failed to pursue actively municipalities for cleanup costs because the average cost to clean up a Superfund site is approximately $25 million. If compelled to pay such a cost, many cities literally would go bankrupt or would have to cut essential services such as police and trash removal. To pay for liability costs, municipalities would have to issue bonds or raise taxes. Both options are problematic because large damage awards could lower a city’s bond ratings, forcing them to borrow to pay for future damages. Hiking taxes assumes a large enough tax base and that a tax proposal could be passed expediently, which is unlikely since private parties want their money immediately.

If the EPA chose to pursue municipalities for cleanup costs, one threat to public health and the environment would be eliminated, but many others would be created as a consequence. Thus, under the decisions in *Transportation Leasing* and *B.F. Goodrich*, the EPA admits municipalities are potentially liable but refuses to go after them.

The EPA's unwillingness to pursue municipalities may create more problems than it solves. CERCLA liability is joint and several, meaning the EPA may pursue one, some, or all responsible parties and order them to clean up the entire site. Private parties can seek a right of contribution from other potentially responsible parties.

Since private parties cannot institute a contribution claim until they incur cleanup costs, those who contribute very little to the contamination may be responsible for the entire cleanup cost. In one instance, the EPA pursued private parties for the entire cleanup cost when their actual contribution was only 0.06 percent, with the remaining waste belonging to the Town of Tyngsboro, Massachusetts.

Private parties will be reluctant to accept contribution awards less than municipalities' full liability costs, and defending such claims could cost municipalities a tremendous amount of money in litigation expenses. Furthermore, if municipalities are held responsible, private parties will be more willing to go after municipal assets. The very purpose behind the EPA's policy of not holding municipalities liable may be defeated under CERCLA's current scheme because corporations often have little pressure to reduce the municipalities' liability.

**2\. The "Arranging for Disposal" Ambiguity**

The current municipal liability scheme is also problematic because courts fail to interpret the phrase "arranging for disposal" uniformly.

* In *Lincoln v. Republic Ecology Corp.*, the court reasoned that CERCLA's history suggests Congress intended to force those who either benefit *financially* or directly contribute to a release to internalize environmental costs. This differs from *Transportation Leasing Co.* and *B.F. Goodrich*, where financial rewards were not considered when determining arranger liability.  
* Other courts, applying CERCLA to state liability, require that the state either *owned* or *possessed* the hazardous substance for which it arranged disposal, and that the state went beyond establishing regulations to directly manage the site.

The interpretation of "arranging for disposal" is far narrower in some contexts (like state liability cases) than in cases like *Transportation Leasing Co.* and *B.F. Goodrich*, and also narrower than the EPA's interpretation. This ambiguity means municipalities lack guidance on avoiding liability, and private parties lack guidance on whether to sue municipalities as arrangers, resulting in unnecessary and protracted litigation that delays landfill remediation.

**3\. Political Infighting**

Political infighting within the executive branch further compounds CERCLA's problems.

* When the EPA proposed regulations requiring liners and ground monitoring systems for new landfills to reduce municipal liability, the Office of Management and Budget ("OMB") objected, believing the rule would impose too high a cost on homeowners ($10 to $15 per year for the average homeowner). A compromise was never worked out.  
* The EPA attempted to apportion liability by calculating the average cleanup cost of a landfill at approximately $2,279,000, with about $94,000 attributable to municipalities. Based on this, the EPA proposed apportioning four percent of the cleanup costs of any municipal landfill to municipalities. White House officials held up the necessary implementing regulations for this proposal.

**4\. Hitting the Taxpayer Too Hard and Too Often**

The court in *B.F. Goodrich* reasoned that taxpayers should bear municipal liability expenses since they benefit from waste disposal. However, a careful analysis suggests taxpayers bear too heavy a burden.

Congress created the Superfund to help finance cleanup costs, but the fund has limited uses. It cannot be used to clean up federal facilities, which rely on separate statutory authority.

The Superfund is also not used for state and local sites. Due to the Supreme Court's decision in *Pennsylvania v. Union Gas Co.* (holding states liable under CERCLA) and the *Transportation Leasing* and *B.F. Goodrich* rulings (holding municipalities liable), taxpayers can now be hit from four directions to pay for hazardous waste cleanup: the Superfund, federal facility cleanups, state cleanups, and municipal cleanups. Since cities and states run on tight budgets, a single, large environmental liability finding against a city could force drastic cuts or elimination of essential services. Thus, even though the taxpayer pays heavily, they may get very little in return.

The current municipal liability scheme under CERCLA is a failure because it offers virtually no guidance for municipalities to avoid liability, despite being required to remove trash. This, combined with the inequitable burden on the tax-paying public, signals a dire need for a new approach.III. THE SOLUTION: A LIMITED MUNICIPAL EXEMPTION

Some members of Congress have proposed measures to exempt municipalities from CERCLA liability. Bills by Senator Frank Lautenberg and Representative Chris Smith, both of New Jersey, would prevent major polluters from suing local governments that send MSW to NPL landfills. Lautenberg argues his bill is aimed at stopping opportunistic lawsuits by industrial polluters looking to share cleanup costs. Smith believes the explosion of industry lawsuits against municipalities is threatening the Superfund program's existence.

To gain wider support, the Lautenberg-Smith proposals should be modified. A more workable solution is to exempt municipalities from liability only when they arranged for the disposal of solid waste or transported it to landfills.

In return for this exemption:

* Private industry would be permitted to seek the municipalities' financial share from the Superfund.  
* Municipalities would have to establish that they sent the waste to the landfill believing in good faith that the landfill was in compliance with environmental laws.

The benefits of such a plan are far-reaching:

* **Efficiency:** The taxpayer would only support one funding source, eliminating much of the bureaucracy and costs of running overlapping programs.  
* **Municipal Services:** Municipalities would not need to reserve funds for defending CERCLA suits, and the money could instead be used for services like education, police, and fire. The good-faith certification requirement would eliminate any possibility of an unjust reward.  
* **Incentive:** The incentive for municipalities not to pollute would be maintained, as the exemption would only apply to activities related to arranging for disposal or transporting solid waste.  
* **Faster Recovery for Industry:** Private parties could pursue the municipality's proportionate share from the Superfund by filing a counterclaim immediately after being sued by the EPA. This would eliminate the need for private parties to incur response costs before instituting claims, greatly easing the burden of litigation.  
* **Quicker Settlements:** Requiring the municipality's share to come from the Superfund would incentivize the EPA to settle cases quickly and efficiently. This would encourage all parties to negotiate a settlement, allowing private parties to devote more resources to remediation instead of exorbitant legal fees. The limited exemption proposal offers more hope of meeting CERCLA's goal of environmental remediation than the current scheme.

IV. CONCLUSION

CERCLA, which is risk-based, uses incentives to reduce the likelihood of hazardous substance releases. While this approach works in most cases, it has little deterrent effect when municipalities arrange for disposal or transport solid waste. Although a theoretical model exists to redirect policy toward a preventive market-based approach with incentives for waste minimization, municipalities have very little practical control over the waste generated by their citizens. Under the current system, municipalities have virtually no way to minimize their liabilities associated with arranging for solid waste disposal and transportation.

The limited exemption proposal offered in this Article would help clean up hazardous municipal landfills while ensuring vital municipal services continue. It would also allow private industry to recover municipalities' proportionate share of cleanup costs more quickly and efficiently. Furthermore, because the incentive to settle cases would increase, court dockets would become less congested, and remediation at hazardous sites would begin much faster than under the current CERCLA scheme. In short, the limited exemption for municipalities proposed in this Article will help meet the goals of CERCLA and ensure that public health and safety will not be jeopardized.-----\* Attorney, Arnelle & Hastie, San Francisco, California.
