# HSRA State Outreach Priority Framework 2026

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-10
**Ticket:** Exploratory — no ticket
**Topic:** State-level signal scoring for HSRA/PopHealthMap outreach prioritization
**Problem Statement:** Rank U.S. states by likelihood of wanting HSRA/PopHealthMap, using public evidence that meaningfully differentiates states from one another.
**Status:** Complete

---

## Summary

CMS Rural Health Transformation (RHT) applies to all 50 states and is therefore a baseline condition, not a differentiator. Meaningful differentiation comes from five signal categories that vary substantially across states: (1) rural burden intensity, (2) health equity infrastructure maturity, (3) cancer-control and screening gap severity, (4) environmental justice screening tool adoption, and (5) PHAB/CHA/CHIP planning activity. States that score high across multiple categories have both the institutional appetite and the operational need for a product like HSRA. States that score high on burden but low on infrastructure are also high-priority because they represent the largest unmet need and are most likely to be seeking tools to fill the gap.

**Top-tier states by composite signal (Tier 1):** Mississippi, West Virginia, Arkansas, Oklahoma, Alabama, Kentucky, Tennessee, Georgia, Louisiana, New Mexico

**Strong secondary tier (Tier 2):** North Carolina, South Carolina, Kansas, Texas, Montana, Wyoming, Idaho, Missouri, Indiana, Ohio

**Active infrastructure tier (Tier 3 — high capacity, moderate burden):** Colorado, Oregon, Minnesota, Maryland, Vermont, Washington, Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Massachusetts

## Scoring Framework

### Dimension 1: Rural Burden Intensity (0–3 pts)

**What it measures:** How much of the state's population and hospital infrastructure is rural, and how severe the health outcomes gap is.

**Evidence sources:**
- USDA ERS Rural-Urban Continuum Codes 2023 (county-level rurality)
- America's Health Rankings 2024 (state health outcome scores)
- Chartis Center for Rural Health, February 2025 (rural hospital vulnerability)
- CDC PLACES 2024 (tract-level chronic disease and screening rates)

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 3 | >40% rural population AND/OR >30% of rural hospitals vulnerable to closure AND bottom-quartile health outcomes |
| 2 | 25–40% rural population OR 20–30% hospital vulnerability OR bottom-half outcomes |
| 1 | 10–25% rural population with moderate outcomes gap |
| 0 | <10% rural population, no significant rural hospital risk |

**Key evidence:**
- Vermont: 66.1% rural population (highest in U.S.) — America's Health Rankings 2024 via americashealthrankings.org
- Mississippi: 49% of rural hospitals vulnerable to closure; ranked 49th for women and children health — Chartis Feb 2025 via chartis.com; America's Health Rankings 2024
- Arkansas: 50% of rural hospitals vulnerable (highest rate in U.S.); ranked last for women and children — Chartis Feb 2025; America's Health Rankings 2024
- Kansas: 47% rural hospital vulnerability rate — Chartis Feb 2025
- Tennessee: 44% rural hospital vulnerability rate — Chartis Feb 2025
- West Virginia, Kentucky, Oklahoma, Louisiana: consistently bottom-5 in senior health and overall outcomes — America's Health Rankings 2024
- Texas: 47 vulnerable rural hospitals (highest absolute count) — Chartis Feb 2025

---

### Dimension 2: Health Equity Infrastructure Maturity (0–3 pts)

**What it measures:** Whether the state has institutional structures (health equity office, dashboard, legislation) that create demand for data tools — but also whether those structures are nascent enough to still need external support.

**Scoring logic:** This dimension has two sub-signals that pull in opposite directions:
- **Demand signal (high):** State has a health equity office, active dashboard, or recent legislation → they are buyers
- **Gap signal (high):** State has high burden but no infrastructure → they are the most urgent need

For HSRA outreach, score highest for states that have *some* infrastructure (enough to have a budget and a buyer) but are not yet self-sufficient.

**Evidence sources:**
- State health equity dashboard inventory (CHIA Massachusetts, Colorado CDPHE, Connecticut DPH, Vermont, Washington HEAL Act, Ohio)
- State health equity legislation tracker (Massachusetts S.901/H.1416, New Jersey S777, Mississippi HB 33, Minnesota MDH HEAL Council, Illinois)
- PHAB accreditation activity 2024–2025 (phaboard.org, cdc.gov)

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 3 | Active health equity office + public dashboard + recent legislation OR PHAB-accredited state health dept with active CHA/CHIP cycle |
| 2 | Health equity office or dashboard exists but no recent legislation; OR PHAB accreditation in progress |
| 1 | Some health equity activity (e.g., one program or initiative) but no formal office or dashboard |
| 0 | No identifiable health equity infrastructure |

**Key evidence:**
- Massachusetts: CHIA health equity dashboard updated Sept 2025; S.901/H.1416 refiled for 2025–2026 session — chiamass.gov; malegislature.gov
- Colorado: CDPHE Health Equity Strategic Plan with quarterly public dashboard updates; EJ Grants Program dashboard launched March 2026 — colorado.gov
- Connecticut: Interactive health equity dashboard combining outcomes with SDOH drivers — ct.gov
- Vermont: New public health equity dashboards published Feb 2025 per legislative mandate — vermont.gov
- Washington: HEAL Act Budget and Equity Dashboard FY2025 — arcgis.com
- Minnesota: MDH Division of Health Equity Strategy and Innovation; HEAL Council active 2024–2025 — health.state.mn.us
- Mississippi: HB 33 (2024) requires State Health Officer to develop health gap closure program by 2030 — legiscan.com
- Alabama: PHAB reaccredited Nov 2024 — cdc.gov
- Arizona: PHAB reaccredited March 2024 — cdc.gov
- Arkansas: PHAB reaccredited Nov 2024 — cdc.gov
- Connecticut: PHAB reaccredited Nov 2024 — cdc.gov
- Kansas: PHAB reaccredited Nov 2024 — cdc.gov
- Rhode Island: PHAB reaccredited 2024 — ri.gov

### Dimension 3: Cancer Control and Screening Gap Severity (0–3 pts)

**What it measures:** Whether the state has documented cancer screening disparities, an active comprehensive cancer control plan, and/or a cancer coalition that would use HSRA-style prioritization.

**Evidence sources:**
- CDC National Comprehensive Cancer Control Program (NCCCP) — all 50 states funded, but plan recency and specificity varies
- State cancer control plan activity: Maryland 2026–2030, Georgia 2024–2029, New Jersey 2021–2025
- CDC/NCI rural cancer screening data: colorectal (61.4% NM to 80.4% RI), cervical (rural 48.6% vs urban 64.0%), breast (rural 72.1% vs urban 77.1%)
- NCI SEER state cancer incidence and mortality data

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 3 | Active cancer control plan (2023–present) + documented rural/racial screening gap + cancer coalition with geographic prioritization mandate |
| 2 | Active plan but limited geographic specificity; OR high screening gap without active coalition |
| 1 | Plan exists but outdated (pre-2022) or no documented rural gap |
| 0 | No identifiable cancer control infrastructure |

**Key evidence:**
- New Mexico: Lowest colorectal screening rate in U.S. at 61.4% — americashealthrankings.org via CDC BRFSS
- Rhode Island: Highest colorectal screening rate at 80.4% — same source (useful as contrast)
- Rural cervical cancer screening: 48.6% vs 64.0% urban (2022 data); largest decline of all cancer screenings 2018–2022 — NIH/CDC via nih.gov
- Rural patients 20% more likely to be diagnosed with late-stage cancers — cancer.gov
- 38 states have fewer oncologists per capita than in 2014 — cancerletter.com (2024 study)
- Maryland: Comprehensive Cancer Control Plan 2026–2030 published Nov 2023 — maryland.gov
- Georgia: Comprehensive Cancer Control Plan 2024–2029 released Aug 2025 — georgiacancerinfo.org
- New Jersey: Plan 2021–2025 active — nj.gov

### Dimension 4: Environmental Justice Screening Tool Adoption (0–3 pts)

**What it measures:** Whether the state has its own EJ screening tool (or is building one), which signals both institutional capacity for geospatial health-risk analysis and a direct workflow gap that HSRA fills.

**Context:** Federal EJScreen was removed from EPA website in February 2025. As of March 2025, at least 16 states had developed their own EJ mapping tools. This creates both a gap (states without tools need one) and a demand signal (states with tools are already buyers of this type of analysis).

**Evidence sources:**
- State EJ tool inventory: California CalEnviroScreen 4.0 (5.0 draft Jan 2026), Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota (Frontline Communities Protection Act 2023), New Jersey, Vermont (mandated by Jan 2025), Colorado (EJ Grants dashboard March 2026), Michigan (EJ impact grants 2024)
- envirodatagov.org inventory of state EJ tools (March 2025)
- climatepolicydashboard.org state EJ policy tracker

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 3 | State has its own EJ screening tool (operational or in active development) with legislative mandate |
| 2 | State uses federal EJScreen or has informal EJ mapping capacity; OR has EJ legislation without a dedicated tool |
| 1 | State has referenced EJ in policy documents but no dedicated tool or mandate |
| 0 | No identifiable EJ screening activity |

**Key evidence:**
- California: CalEnviroScreen 4.0 operational; 5.0 draft announced Jan 28, 2026 — ca.gov
- Massachusetts: State-level EJ tool similar to CalEnviroScreen; cumulative impact law — smartcitiesdive.com
- New Jersey: Cumulative impact law with EJ screening requirement — smartcitiesdive.com
- Minnesota: 2023 Frontline Communities Protection Act requires EJ screening for environmental analyses — smartcitiesdive.com
- Vermont: Agency of Natural Resources mandated to develop EJ mapping tool by Jan 2025 — envirodatagov.org
- Colorado: EJ Grants Program dashboard launched March 5, 2026 — colorado.gov
- Michigan: EJ impact grants program and Justice40 accelerator 2024 — envirodatagov.org
- Maryland: State-level EJ tool noted as similar to former federal EJScreen — smartcitiesdive.com
- 16+ states with own tools as of March 2025 — envirodatagov.org

### Dimension 5: CHA/CHIP/PHAB Planning Activity and Rural Health Strategic Plans (0–3 pts)

**What it measures:** Whether the state has active, recent community health assessment and improvement planning infrastructure that would use HSRA outputs as evidence.

**Evidence sources:**
- PHAB accreditation activity 2024–2025 (phaboard.org)
- State rural health strategic plans: Pennsylvania 2024–2027, Maryland 2025–2030, Arizona 2024–2025, North Carolina RHTP, Iowa 2024–2026 CHIP
- NASHP 2025 Annual Conference state rural health transformation plan submissions
- State CHA/CHIP examples: Oregon Healthier Together 2020–2024, Pennsylvania Rural Health Association 2023–2028, Vermont Northwestern Medical Center 2024–2025

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 3 | PHAB-accredited state health dept + active rural health strategic plan (2023–present) + published CHA/CHIP with rural equity priorities |
| 2 | PHAB accreditation OR active rural health plan, but not both |
| 1 | CHA/CHIP exists but rural equity is not a named priority |
| 0 | No identifiable CHA/CHIP or rural health planning activity |

**Key evidence:**
- Pennsylvania: Office of Rural Health Strategic Plan 2024–2027 published — psu.edu
- Maryland: 2025–2030 Rural Health Strategic Plan published — maryland.gov
- Arizona: Health Improvement Plan 2024–2025 Update addresses rural health and SDOH — arizona.gov
- Iowa: 2024–2026 CHIP for 14-county region with SDOH and health equity focus — iowa.gov
- Oregon: Healthier Together 2020–2024 names rural residents as priority equity population — dhsoha.state.or.us
- North Carolina: Rural health action plan + RHTP innovation focus — nashp.org
- Vermont: Northwestern Medical Center 2024–2025 CHNA with rural equity focus — northwesternmedicalcenter.org
- PHAB state-level reaccreditations 2024: Alabama, Arizona, Arkansas, Connecticut, Kansas, Rhode Island — cdc.gov; phaboard.org

### Dimension 6: Medicaid Non-Expansion / Coverage Gap Pressure (0–2 pts, modifier)

**What it measures:** Whether the state has not expanded Medicaid, creating a coverage gap that intensifies rural hospital financial pressure and creates urgency for population health tools.

**Rationale:** Non-expansion states have higher uncompensated care burdens, higher rural hospital closure risk, and stronger financial incentive to use data tools to justify targeted investments. 74% of rural hospital closures have occurred in non-expansion states. This is a modifier (max 2 pts) because it amplifies other signals rather than standing alone.

**Evidence sources:**
- KFF Medicaid expansion status tracker (kff.org)
- Rural hospital closure correlation: 74% in non-expansion states — healthlaw.org; nih.gov
- Rural hospitals in expansion states 62% less likely to close — ajmc.com

**Non-expansion states as of early 2026:**
Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming
(Note: Wisconsin expanded to 100% FPL but not full ACA expansion; North Carolina enacted expansion but implementation timing uncertain)

## Signal Category Reference Guide

### How to Use This Framework for Outreach

**Tier 1 states:** Lead with rural hospital crisis + cancer screening gap + CMS RHT implementation pressure. The pitch is: "You have the funding (RHT), the urgency (hospital closures, screening gaps), and now you need the evidence layer to allocate it defensibly."

**Tier 2 states:** Lead with rural burden + non-expansion coverage gap. The pitch is: "Your rural hospitals are at risk, your coverage gap is real, and you need to show where to concentrate limited resources."

**Tier 3 states:** Lead with health equity infrastructure + EJ tool gap (especially post-EJScreen removal). The pitch is: "You have the institutional appetite and the mandate. HSRA gives you the air-toxics + SDOH + cancer-risk integration that your existing dashboard doesn't have."

## What Does NOT Differentiate States (Baseline Conditions)

The following apply to all 50 states and should not be used as differentiators:

| Condition | Why It's a Baseline |
|-----------|---------------------|
| CMS Rural Health Transformation Program participation | All 50 states applied and received awards (Dec 2025) |
| CDC NCCCP cancer control funding | All 50 states + DC + 8 territories funded |
| CDC PLACES data availability | All counties and census tracts covered nationally |
| HRSA rural health designation (HPSA/MUA) | Exists in every state |
| PHAB accreditation *availability* | Open to all; what matters is whether the state has pursued it |

## Data Gaps and Caveats

1. **PHAB state-level list is incomplete.** The full list of currently accredited *state* health departments (as opposed to local/county) is not publicly consolidated in a single document. The phaboard.org Excel file is the authoritative source but requires direct access. The evidence above reflects partial data from CDC and press releases.

2. **EJ tool inventory is a moving target.** The federal EJScreen removal in Feb 2025 is accelerating state-level tool development. The "16 states" figure is from March 2025 (envirodatagov.org) and is likely already outdated.

3. **Cancer control plan recency varies.** CDC NCCCP funds all states, but plan quality, recency, and geographic specificity vary enormously. The plans cited (Maryland, Georgia, New Jersey) are the ones with publicly documented recent activity. Many states have plans that are technically active but not recently updated.

4. **Medicaid expansion status is in flux.** Several states have trigger laws that could end expansion if federal FMAP changes. Wisconsin's partial expansion and North Carolina's implementation timing add uncertainty.

5. **Composite scores are directional, not precise.** The scoring rubric is designed to differentiate states, not to produce exact rankings. Two states with the same score may have very different buyer profiles.
