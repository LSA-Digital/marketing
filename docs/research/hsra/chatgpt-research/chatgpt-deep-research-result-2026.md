## 1. Executive Summary

**Research question.** What external facts most strongly support HSRA’s market, competitor, funding, and replication thesis without overstating the current product?

**Key findings**

* The strongest nationwide demand signal is the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program: CMS describes it as a $50 billion program, all 50 states received awards in December 2025, and the NOFO requires each initiative to include at least four quantifiable metrics with at least one metric reported at county or community level. ([CMS][1])
* The public federal data ingredients relevant to HSRA already exist at county/tract scale, but they remain fragmented across separate systems: CDC PLACES publishes health and health-related data at county, place, census tract, and ZCTA levels; CDC/ATSDR SVI ranks vulnerability at tract level; CDC/ATSDR’s Environmental Justice Index measures cumulative environmental burden through a health-equity lens; NCI/CDC State Cancer Profiles provides geography-based cancer burden and now includes county-level modeled estimates for several screening/risk measures. ([CDC][2])
* The compliance/reporting environment is real and recurring: PHAB Version 2022 remains the official accreditation guidance; NACCHO’s MAPP 2.0 remains a current framework for community health improvement; IRS section 501(r)(3) still requires nonprofit hospitals to conduct a CHNA every three years and adopt an implementation strategy. ([Public Health Accreditation Board][3])
* Multiple states already use tract-level burden or disparity tools for targeting investment, permitting, or prioritization. California targets disadvantaged communities for investment using CalEnviroScreen; Washington’s Environmental Health Disparities Map compares census tracts to identify where more attention is needed; Maryland’s MDEnviroScreen includes EJ scores on permit applications; Colorado says its mapping tools help direct funding and resources to the communities that need them most. ([OEHHA][4])
* Adjacent markets normalize a commercialization model of software plus configuration/services, with optional premium data. Socially Determined pairs its platform with an advisory team; Esri pairs ArcGIS with Professional Services and advisory programs; SparkMap sells subscription-tiered community-assessment indicators and reports; Claritas and Esri both monetize premium demographic/behavioral/location data. ([Socially Determined][5])

**Core sources**

* CMS RHT overview, awards announcement, and NOFO. ([CMS][6])
* CDC PLACES, SVI, EJI; NCI/CDC State Cancer Profiles. ([CDC][2])
* PHAB, NACCHO, IRS. ([Public Health Accreditation Board][3])
* California, Washington, Maryland, Colorado state EJ tools. ([OEHHA][4])
* Vendor sites for Socially Determined, Esri, SparkMap, Claritas. ([Socially Determined][5])

**Buyer/program implications**

* **[Configurable with services]** The best external support is for a state-first, program-specific deployment story: county/community metric support for RHT, CHNA/CHA packaging, EJ/community targeting, and state-specific reporting layers. ([CMS][1])
* **[Premium extension]** Optional enrichment for outreach, segmentation, and intervention design has strong external analogs, but should stay separate from the public-data baseline in HSRA messaging.  ([Socially Determined][5])
* **[Positioning / strategy thesis]** The public evidence supports a repeatable problem shape across states, not proof that every state will buy the same package. ([CMS][1])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “The clearest current 50-state demand signal is the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program, which funds every state and requires county- or community-level outcomes measurement.” ([CMS][1])
* “Based on the public sources reviewed, the relevant federal data building blocks exist nationally but remain split across separate health, vulnerability, environmental-burden, and cancer-burden tools.” ([CDC][2])

**More thesis than proof**

* “Because the demand drivers are national, all 50 states should adopt the same HSRA package.”
* “Premium enrichment is a default requirement for most public-sector buyers.”

## 2. Competitor Landscape

**Research question.** Which external categories are most relevant to HSRA, and what do they appear to do well versus not do end-to-end?

**Key findings**

* **Population health / government analytics.** Arcadia presents itself as a one-stop shop for population health insights and care-management targeting; Innovaccer markets Medicaid and public-health analytics, real-time oversight, and data unification for state and county programs. Their public positioning is strongest around clinical/program analytics, interoperability, case management, and oversight. The reviewed pages did not describe a single out-of-the-box workflow that combines environmental burden, formal air-toxics risk logic, and tract-level resource-allocation support. ([Arcadia][7])
* **SDOH / social-care platforms.** Unite Us and findhelp are strongest on screening, referrals, closed-loop workflows, and service-network coordination; Socially Determined is strongest on social-risk analytics plus advisory support. Their public positioning is strong for social-risk identification and intervention orchestration, but the reviewed materials are not centered on pollutant-specific exposure burden or formal HRA methodology. ([uniteus.com][8])
* **CHNA / community assessment tools.** Community Commons and SparkMap are strong on community-assessment data access, interactive mapping, custom downloadable reports, and subscription-tier indicator libraries. The public descriptions emphasize assessment/reporting and case-making, not a unified environmental-burden plus health-risk engine. ([Community Commons][9])
* **GIS / EJ / location intelligence.** Esri is strong on geospatial infrastructure, spatial analysis, health-equity mapping, and premium demographic/location data. State screening tools such as CalEnviroScreen, Washington’s EHD Map, and MDEnviroScreen are strong on tract-based burden ranking and prioritization. These are strong analogs for mapping and prioritization, but the public descriptions reviewed do not establish a single, standardized end-to-end workflow spanning pollution burden, cancer burden, SDOH, and auditable resource-allocation logic in one product narrative. ([Esri][10])

**Core sources**

* Arcadia, Innovaccer Government. ([Arcadia][7])
* Unite Us, findhelp, Socially Determined. ([uniteus.com][8])
* Community Commons, SparkMap. ([Community Commons][9])
* Esri plus state screening tools. ([Esri][10])

**Buyer/program implications**

* **[Configurable with services]** The defensible competitive frame is an **integration gap**: many adjacent tools cover important pieces, but public evidence is stronger for separate categories than for a single end-to-end tract/county prioritization stack. ([Arcadia][7])
* HSRA should not be pitched as a replacement for care-management systems, referral networks, or full GIS platforms; it is better framed as a decision-support layer that can sit between public-health planning, EJ screening, and resource allocation. That caution is consistent with your local competitive-landscape note.  ([Innovaccer][11])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “The closest competitors are adjacent categories rather than one-for-one substitutes: population-health analytics, social-care platforms, CHNA/community-assessment tools, and GIS/EJ screening tools.” ([Arcadia][7])
* “The public materials reviewed suggest an integration opportunity, not a claim that no competitor can do any similar task.”  ([Arcadia][7])

**More thesis than proof**

* “No competitor can combine these domains.”
* “HSRA replaces enterprise PHM, social-care referral, CHNA reporting, and GIS platforms.”

## 3. Government / Public-Sector Demand Signals

**Research question.** What current public-sector signals suggest demand for tract/county products that combine environmental burden, social vulnerability, and resource-allocation support?

**Key findings**

* CMS RHT is the cleanest 50-state demand signal. All 50 states received awards, and the NOFO requires measurable outcomes with county/community granularity. That means every state now has a federally funded reason to organize rural-health initiatives around place-based measurement. ([CMS][1])
* CDC PLACES, SVI, and EJI show that public agencies already work with tract- and county-level health and vulnerability data. PLACES publishes health and health-related data at county, place, tract, and ZCTA levels; SVI supports tract-level vulnerability mapping within counties; EJI is designed to identify communities most at risk from cumulative environmental burdens. ([CDC][2])
* State and local agencies are also buying adjacent analytics and dashboard work. Connecticut’s Health Equity Dashboards were developed for the Office of Health Strategy by Onpoint Health Data using ARPA funds; Kentucky’s Health Equity Dashboard was developed with Milliman and Day Health Strategies; Brunswick County, North Carolina issued an RFP for collaborative CHNA/CHA data collection, analysis, and reporting. These are not HSRA-equivalent procurements, but they are concrete evidence that public buyers fund configured health-equity and assessment analytics. ([CT.gov][12])
* State EJ tools strengthen the case that tract-level burden ranking is already operational in government. California uses CalEnviroScreen for targeted investment, Washington uses census-tract comparisons to direct attention, Maryland includes EJ scores on permit applications, and Colorado says its mapping tools direct funding and resources to disproportionately impacted communities. ([OEHHA][4])

**Core sources**

* CMS RHT public materials. ([CMS][1])
* CDC PLACES, SVI, EJI. ([CDC][2])
* Connecticut, Kentucky, Brunswick County public materials. ([CT.gov][12])
* CA/WA/MD/CO official EJ pages. ([OEHHA][4])

**Buyer/program implications**

* **[Configurable with services]** The strongest near-term public buyers are state Medicaid/rural-transformation teams, state and local health departments, and hospital/community-benefit groups that need defensible place-based prioritization rather than a generic dashboard. ([CMS][1])
* The public evidence is stronger for **programmatic demand plus configured procurements** than for a mature, nationally standardized software category labeled exactly like HSRA. ([CMS][1])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “Public-sector demand is strongest where funding, reporting, or prioritization requires county- or tract-level evidence, especially in rural transformation, CHNA/CHA work, and state EJ or health-equity programs.” ([CMS][1])
* “Illustrative public procurements show agencies are willing to buy configured health-equity dashboards and assessment support, even when the category is not labeled as an integrated environmental-health platform.” ([CT.gov][12])

**More thesis than proof**

* “The existence of adjacent dashboard procurements proves direct HSRA demand in every state.”

## 4. Rural Health Funding / Compliance Drivers

**Research question.** Which federal/state programs, mandates, accreditation frameworks, or reporting requirements are most relevant to this product category?

**Key findings**

* **CMS Rural Health Transformation** is the largest obvious funding driver. CMS describes RHT as a five-year, $50 billion rural-health investment, and the NOFO ties funding to measurable initiative-level outcomes at county/community granularity. ([CMS][1])
* **HRSA/FORHP rural-health programs** create recurring planning and funding pressure. HRSA says FORHP administers local and state grants, supports all 50 State Offices of Rural Health, offers community-based rural programs, and provides technical assistance to rural hospitals. HRSA also funds SHIP for hardware/software/training and TTAP for technical assistance to rural hospitals. ([HRSA][13])
* **HRSA data tools** reinforce place-based planning. HRSA’s data warehouse points applicants to tools like GeoCare Navigator, the Unmet Need Score Map, and the High Need Mapping Tool to identify high-need regions and service-area needs. ([HRSA Data Portal][14])
* **PHAB accreditation** and **NACCHO MAPP 2.0** create an assessment-and-improvement cycle for public health departments. PHAB Version 2022 is still the official accreditation guidance, and MAPP 2.0 remains NACCHO’s community-driven framework for community health improvement. ([Public Health Accreditation Board][3])
* **IRS 501(r)(3)** keeps CHNA work on a recurring three-year cycle for nonprofit hospitals, with an implementation-strategy requirement and Schedule H reporting. ([IRS][15])
* **Cancer-control and rural-disparity infrastructure** also matters. NCI/CDC State Cancer Profiles exists specifically for health planners and policy makers to prioritize cancer-control investments, and NCI says rural populations have higher average cancer death rates, especially for screening-preventable cancers. ([State Cancer Profiles][16])
* **State EJ / environmental-health drivers** matter where investment or permitting decisions already reference tract-based burden screening. California, Washington, Maryland, and Colorado all show that pattern publicly. ([OEHHA][4])

**Core sources**

* CMS, HRSA, PHAB, NACCHO, IRS, NCI/CDC, and official state EJ pages. ([CMS][1])

**Buyer/program implications**

* **[Configurable with services]** The product category maps well to agencies and organizations that need grant planning, county/community metric tracking, community health assessment, cancer-control prioritization, or EJ-related targeting. ([CMS][1])
* **[Positioning / strategy thesis]** The cleanest story is not “a new dashboard market,” but “a cross-program decision-support layer that helps buyers connect environmental burden, vulnerability, and intervention prioritization under existing funding and compliance pressure.” ([CMS][1])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “This product category aligns with both funded programs and recurring compliance/reporting cycles: CMS rural transformation, HRSA rural grants and planning tools, PHAB accreditation, nonprofit-hospital CHNAs, and state EJ screening programs.” ([CMS][1])
* “Rural cancer-control disparities strengthen the case for place-based prioritization, especially where screening-preventable cancers and access barriers overlap.” ([Cancer Control][17])

**More thesis than proof**

* “Any single one of these funding/compliance drivers is enough to close a sale.”
* “All rural-health grant applicants will need a combined pollution-plus-SDOH product.”

## 5. Tennessee Replication Evidence

**Research question.** What public evidence suggests that a Tennessee-style public-health allocation use case could matter in other states, even if adoption is not yet proven?

**Key findings**

* The underlying public-data pattern is national, not Tennessee-specific. PLACES, SVI, EJI, State Cancer Profiles, and HRSA mapping tools all operate across the U.S. and at county/tract or similarly local levels. That makes the *data structure* of a Tennessee-style use case replicable even when local policy framing differs. ([CDC][2])
* The **decision pressure** is also national. CMS RHT now funds all 50 states and requires county/community metrics; PHAB and CHNA cycles keep local prioritization active; NCI highlights rural cancer disparities and screening-preventable burden. ([CMS][1])
* State EJ and health-disparity tools show that tract-level burden ranking is already accepted in several states as a basis for targeting attention, resources, investments, or permit-related review. California, Washington, Maryland, and Colorado are the clearest public examples found. ([OEHHA][4])
* The internal Tennessee framing aligns with this public pattern, but your own local evidence register is right to treat Tennessee as an anchor business case or pilot frame, not proof of generalized statewide deployment elsewhere.  

**Core sources**

* CDC/ATSDR, NCI/CDC, HRSA, CMS, PHAB/IRS, and the internal Tennessee boundary notes. ([CDC][2]) 

**Buyer/program implications**

* **[Configurable with services]** The most plausible replication motion is a **state-first configuration pattern**, not a single national rollout package. The state targets with the best fit are likely those with active rural-transformation funding, visible sub-state cancer disparities, current CHNA/CHA pressure, or active state EJ/disparity tools. ([CMS][1])
* **[Positioning / strategy thesis]** Tennessee is best used as an example of a wider decision problem that already appears in public policy and public data elsewhere.  ([CDC][2])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “The external evidence supports repeatable problem shape, not validated adoption outside Tennessee.”

* “Tennessee demonstrates near-term national scalability.”
* “Because state data exist nationally, adoption risk is low.”

## 8. Open Questions / Weak Evidence

**Research question.** Where is the evidence still thin enough that wording should stay cautious?

**Key findings**

* A full 50-state procurement census for this exact product shape is hard to substantiate publicly. The public evidence is strongest for nationwide **programmatic demand** plus illustrative configured procurements, not for a single, standardized procurement category. ([CMS][1])
* Competitor “gaps” are inferred from public product descriptions, not audited across every module, custom deployment, or statement of work. That supports category-level positioning, not absolute exclusion claims. ([Arcadia][7])
* Public cancer-control data are strong at state/county level, but sub-county cancer integration is less consistently public. Because the most accessible national cancer-burden and screening tools are county oriented, finer-grained cancer integration will often depend on local data access or services. ([State Cancer Profiles][16])
* State policy and procurement conditions vary widely. The same public-data pattern appears nationally, but the operational buying motion will differ across Medicaid, health department, hospital, and environmental agency channels. ([CMS][1])
* External analogs support a premium-data upsell model, but they do not prove willingness to pay for a specific HPR-like enrichment package in this category. ([Claritas LLC][22])
* Tennessee remains the cleanest anchor narrative, but the local evidence register is correct that it should still be treated as directional rather than as proof of national adoption. 

**Core sources**

* CMS, public procurement examples, competitor sites, NCI/CDC cancer tools, and your local evidence register. ([CMS][1]) 

**Buyer/program implications**

* Keep the external story centered on **demand drivers, repeatable problem shape, and configuration-led deployment**, not on blanket national adoption or default premium-data scope.   ([CMS][1])

**Safe to cite directly**

* “The evidence is strongest for a repeatable public-sector problem and adjacent purchasing behavior, not for a completed national category rollout.” ([CMS][1])
* “Competitor comparisons should stay at category level unless there is direct feature-level evidence.”  ([Arcadia][7])

**More thesis than proof**

* “Procurement friction will be low in most states.”
* “A premium-data bundle will obviously increase win rate.”
* “Tennessee is sufficient proof for a national case study deck.”

This external evidence base supports a careful positioning: **HSRA is best framed as a configurable, auditable prioritization layer that fits real public-sector funding and reporting pressure, while premium enrichment remains optional and Tennessee remains an anchor case, not national proof.**   ([CMS][1])
