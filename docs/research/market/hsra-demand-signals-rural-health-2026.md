## Summary

There is strong, documented, federally-driven demand for county/tract-level analysis of pollution, cancer, and social health factors across all 50 states. Eight distinct federal programs and mandates create concrete, funded reasons for state and local agencies to need exactly what HSRA provides. The critical market insight: **no single federal data source integrates pollution burden + cancer outcomes + social determinants at the census tract level** — that is HSRA's unique position. The $50B CMS Rural Health Transformation Program (FY2026) is the single largest near-term demand driver, with all 50 states now holding active awards requiring county-level outcome metrics.

## Federal Programs & Mandates

### 1. CMS Rural Health Transformation (RHT) Program
**Scale:** $50B / 5 years, all 50 states, $147M–$281M per state in Year 1 (FY2026)  
**Source:** cms.gov, hhs.gov

**What it requires:**
- State plans must include ≥4 quantifiable metrics; ≥1 outcome metric at **county or community level**
- Must address root causes of disease, preventive health, rural health innovations
- CMS Office of Rural Health Transformation monitors implementation

**Why states need HSRA:**
- County-level health baselines required to justify plans and measure progress
- Pollution burden, cancer rates, SDOH are the "root cause" indicators CMS expects
- Texas "Rural Texas Strong" initiative: AI-driven county-level outcome prediction

**Buyer Persona — State Medicaid Director / Rural Health Coordinator:**
- "Which counties have the worst combined pollution + cancer + poverty burden?"
- "Where should we target preventive care to move the needle on rural mortality?"
- "How do we demonstrate measurable county-level improvement to CMS?"

### 2. HRSA Rural Health Grant Programs
**Source:** hrsa.gov, ruralhealthinfo.org, ruralcenter.org

| Program | Focus | Data Need |
|---|---|---|
| Rural Residency Planning & Development (RRPD) | Physician workforce | County-level shortage mapping |
| Rural Communities Opioid Response (RCORP) | Substance use disorder | Tract-level social risk + health outcomes |
| Rural Health Network Development Planning | Integrated care networks | Multi-county disparity analysis |
| Rural Community Health Support | Community-based org capacity | Local SDOH + health burden data |
| Rural Health Clinic Technical Assistance | RHC policy/clinical | County-level access + outcome data |

**CHNA requirements for HRSA applicants:**
- Must collect and analyze county-specific health data: demographics, outcomes, access, SDOH
- Must reference: Census Bureau, County Health Rankings, HRSA GeoCare Navigator
- HRSA provides county-level rural health data and mapping — HSRA extends this

**Buyer Persona — HRSA Grant Applicant (Rural Hospital, FQHC, State Office of Rural Health):**
- "What is the cancer burden in our service area vs. state average?"
- "Which census tracts have the highest combined pollution + poverty + poor health outcomes?"
- "How do we document unmet need to strengthen our grant application?"

### 3. CDC PLACES Program — Data Infrastructure Demand
**Source:** cdc.gov (PLACES 2025 release, updated December 1, 2025)

**What PLACES provides:**
- 40 health measures at county, place, census tract, and ZCTA levels
- Cancer prevalence, colorectal cancer screening, mammography
- 9 SDOH measures (poverty, education, unemployment, housing cost burden, single-parent households)
- 2025 release: 2023 BRFSS data + ACS 2019–2023

**The gap HSRA fills:**
CDC PLACES covers health outcomes + SDOH but does **not** integrate environmental pollution exposure. HSRA provides the integrated view agencies cannot assemble themselves.

**Buyer Persona — State/Local Health Department Epidemiologist:**
- "Where do high cancer rates overlap with high pollution exposure AND high poverty?"
- "Which tracts are environmental justice communities with compounding health burdens?"
- "How do we prioritize screening programs when resources are limited?"

### 4. PHAB Public Health Accreditation
**Source:** phaboard.org, naccho.org  
**Standard:** Domain 1, Standard 1.1 — Community Health Assessment

**Requirements:**
- CHAs must describe health issues and their **distribution**, addressing **disparities between populations or areas**
- Must analyze contributing factors including **environmental factors**
- PHAB Version 2022 active through 2025–2026

**Why sub-county data is required:**
County-level averages mask the disparities PHAB requires agencies to document. Tract-level data is functionally necessary to meet Standard 1.1.

**Buyer Persona — Local Health Department Director:**
- "Which neighborhoods have disproportionate environmental burden AND worse health outcomes?"
- "How do we document environmental contributing factors to health disparities for our CHA?"
- "Can we show PHAB reviewers a data-driven disparity analysis at the sub-county level?"

### 5. ACA-Mandated Nonprofit Hospital CHNAs
**Source:** irs.gov, aha.org, chausa.org  
**Scale:** ~3,000 nonprofit hospitals, triennial cycle

**Requirements:**
- CHNA every 3 years to maintain tax-exempt status (IRS Form 990 Schedule H)
- Must identify significant health needs, solicit input from underserved/low-income/minority populations
- New York State (effective Oct 1, 2025): annual state reporting; first submission due July 1, 2026

**Cancer + pollution in CHNAs:**
- Cancer is a top health concern in most CHNAs
- Community benefit programs can address environmental health (air, water, chemical exposure)
- Pollution recognized as SDOH in rural communities
- 2025 CHNAs from Texas Health, Marshfield Clinic, Intermountain Health, Lexington Health all identify SDOH as key intervention areas

**Buyer Persona — Hospital Community Benefit Director / Population Health VP:**
- "What are the top health needs in our service area at the zip/tract level?"
- "Where do cancer rates, pollution exposure, and poverty converge in our community?"
- "How do we demonstrate our community benefit investments target the highest-need areas?"

### 6. State Environmental Justice Screening Programs
**Context:** Federal EJScreen removed from EPA website early 2025; EPA Office of Environmental Justice shut down  
**Source:** wastedive.com, harvard.edu, envirodatagov.org

**State programs filling the void:**
- **Colorado EnviroScreen 2.0**: Combined environmental stressors — used in oil and gas permitting
- Multiple states maintaining/expanding own EJ screening tools
- States with active EJ legislation (CA, NY, NJ, WA, MN, IL, etc.) still require tract-level EJ analysis

**Why this creates HSRA demand:**
With federal EJScreen gone, states need alternative tools for tract-level pollution + health + demographic analysis. HSRA can serve as a state-deployable replacement or complement.

**Buyer Persona — State Environmental Agency / Office of Environmental Justice:**
- "Which census tracts have the highest combined pollution burden and health vulnerability?"
- "How do we identify environmental justice communities for permitting decisions without EJScreen?"
- "Where should we prioritize remediation and community health investment?"

### 7. ASTHO / NACCHO Community Health Assessment Frameworks
**Source:** astho.org, naccho.org

**Active frameworks:**
- **NACCHO MAPP 2.0** (updated 2023, modified Sept 2025): Community Status Assessment, Community Context Assessment
- **ASTHO State Health Assessment Guidance**: Health status data collection, disparity analysis
- Both explicitly address: SDOH, rural health, environmental health (water/air quality, pollution), cancer

**Rural SDOH Toolkit** explicitly lists: income, employment, education, **environmental health (water and air quality, pollution)**, housing, transportation, food access, community infrastructure.

**Buyer Persona — State Health Assessment Coordinator:**
- "How do we conduct a MAPP 2.0 Community Status Assessment with environmental health data?"
- "Which rural counties need the most targeted SDOH intervention?"
- "How do we align our state health assessment with PHAB accreditation requirements?"

### 8. State Cancer Registry + Environmental Health Integration
**Source:** cancerregistryeducation.org, nih.gov, cancer.gov, cdc.gov

**Use cases (2025–2026):**
- Linking cancer registry data with socio-demographic + geographic indicators to highlight disparities
- Integrating cancer registry data with environmental exposure data to evaluate ecological relationships
- Directing prevention activities to communities based on cancer burden + risk factors
- Data modernization: expanding registries to include screening data, molecular characteristics

**Key research finding:**
Cancer risk from industrial air pollution in majority-Black neighborhoods can be **more than double** that in majority-white neighborhoods. Tracts with higher socio-environmental injustice have **lower screening rates AND higher cancer mortality**.

**Buyer Persona — State Cancer Control Program Director / State Epidemiologist:**
- "Which counties/tracts have cancer rates that correlate with pollution exposure?"
- "Where are cancer screening rates lowest AND pollution burden highest?"
- "How do we target NCI Cancer Control grants to highest-need communities?"

## Key Insight: The Integration Gap

Every program above needs data that crosses three domains simultaneously:
1. **Environmental pollution burden** (air toxics, water quality, industrial proximity)
2. **Cancer and chronic disease outcomes** (incidence, mortality, screening rates)
3. **Social determinants / health equity** (poverty, race, housing, access)

No single federal data source provides all three at the census tract level:
- CDC PLACES: health + SDOH, **no pollution**
- EJScreen (removed): pollution + demographics, **no health outcomes**
- State cancer registries: cancer, **no pollution or SDOH integration**

**HSRA's market position: the integrated county/tract-level view that no federal data source provides.**

## Conclusion & Recommendations

**Recommended Approach:** Lead HSRA sales conversations with the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program as the primary entry point — all 50 states have active awards, county-level outcome metrics are required, and the program runs through FY2030. Secondary entry points: HRSA grant applicants (rural hospitals, FQHCs) and PHAB-accreditation-seeking local health departments.

**Confidence Level:** High — all demand signals are anchored to active federal programs with documented requirements.

**Key Risks / Open Questions:**
- Federal EJScreen rollback reduces one demand driver but creates a replacement opportunity
- CMS RHT Program is new (FY2026) — state implementation timelines vary
- HRSA NOFO specifics for 2026 not yet published; requirements inferred from current practice
