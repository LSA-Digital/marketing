For a long time, we have relied on environmental systems that wait for failure, then argue about penalties. In my 1994 law review article, *Trashing the Town and Making It Pay*, I made the point that punishment and liability fights alone usually come too late.

The better approach is to create incentives that reward prevention before the damage is done. When companies have a reason to reduce risk early, and communities can see real benefit early, you get a more practical path than endless conflict after the fact. The system starts working upstream instead of waiting for a breakdown.

That is the point of LSARS and our community trust approach: Better incentives do not replace accountability. They make better outcomes more likely before trust collapses, before cleanup costs escalate, and before the conversation turns into blame instead of problem-solving.

If you are struggling with community trust and conflict, no matter which side you're on, let's talk.
